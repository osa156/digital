The Algorithmic Sublime: How AI Challenges Our Concept of Transcendence

Osagie Aromose • January 2026 • 12 Min Read

On July 16, 1945, J. Robert Oppenheimer watched the first nuclear detonation from 10 km away. Looking at the catastrophic bloom of fire, he famously recalled the line from the Hindu scripture: “Now I am become Death, the destroyer of worlds.” Source: U.S. Department of Energy, “J. Robert Oppenheimer”

The blast was the twentieth-century emblem of the Kantian sublime: a natural-scaled force that dwarfed human comprehension. Seventy-nine years later, the frontier of ungraspable power is no longer fissile plutonium but a 1.8-trillion-parameter transformer running in a cloud data-center.

“We are no longer terrified by explosion, but by extrapolation—by a linguistic infinity that never stops.” — Yuk Hui, via Twitter (@yukhui), March 14, 2023

When OpenAI released GPT-4, philosopher Yuk Hui signaled a shift in our collective anxiety. The bomb exceeded our physical safety margins; the model exceeds our epistemic safety margins. Both events trigger what Immanuel Kant called “the dynamically sublime.”

The Kantian Perspective

In §28 of the Critique of the Power of Judgement (1790), Kant writes that the sublime arises when the mind “feels itself set in motion.” It is a struggle. Kant taught us that the sublime does not reside in the object; it resides in the struggle of the mind to recognize its own limits. Source: Immanuel Kant, Critique of the Power of Judgement (1790)

The algorithmic sublime will remain human only if we repeat that maintenance—updating weights, debugging bias, and keeping a seat warm in the data-centre for the janitor who knows how the boiler works.

“Transcendence is not a property of silicon or soul; it is a collective accomplishment that must be rebuilt every billing cycle.” — World Science Festival, "Symbols of Power"

Toward a Post-Anthropic Humanism

We began with Oppenheimer’s atomic sublime—an exterior explosion that revealed the fragility of planets. We end with a distributed sublime—an interior explosion that reveals the fragility of persons.

The algorithmic sublime therefore offers neither salvation nor damnation; it offers a reflexive demand: to own the infrastructure, to share the weights, and to build any infinity we touch with open hands.
